Question 1
Explain what HR management is and how it relates to the management process.
Definition
Human Resource Management (HRM) is the process of acquiring, training, appraising, and compensating employees, while attending to their labor relations, health/safety, and fairness concerns. (Dessler, 16e)
Key points
  • HRM is part of the management process. Every manager performs 5 functions: Planning, Organizing, Staffing, Leading, and Controlling.
  • Staffing = HRM. The staffing function involves recruiting, selecting, training, and evaluating employees — all HRM activities.
  • HRM supports all other functions. Without the right people, planning and organizing cannot succeed.
  • Line managers do HRM too. Even non-HR managers hire, train, evaluate, and motivate employees daily.
  • Strategic HRM: HRM links people practices to business goals — e.g., hiring people who support the company's growth strategy.
Examples
Example 1
A store manager who interviews and selects a new cashier is performing an HRM function (staffing) as part of the management process.
Example 2
A CEO planning to expand globally must ensure HR recruits employees with international skills — linking HRM to overall planning.
HRM is not just an HR department's job — it is embedded in every management function and is essential for organizational success.
Question 2
Give examples of how HR management concepts and techniques can be used by all managers.
Introduction
Dessler argues that HRM is not limited to HR specialists. Every manager — finance, sales, operations — uses HR concepts daily to manage people effectively.
Key HR techniques used by all managers
  • Job analysis: Managers define job duties and requirements when assigning roles to team members.
  • Interviewing: Managers conduct interviews to select the best candidates for their teams.
  • Training & orientation: Managers onboard and train new employees to meet performance standards.
  • Performance appraisal: Managers evaluate employees, provide feedback, and decide on promotions.
  • Motivation & fairness: Managers use equity, recognition, and communication to keep employees engaged.
  • Legal compliance: All managers must follow EEO laws to avoid discrimination in hiring/firing.
Examples
Example 1
A sales manager who sets performance targets, gives quarterly reviews, and recommends raises is applying appraisal and compensation HRM techniques.
Example 2
An IT manager who conducts structured interviews and checks references before hiring a programmer is using HRM selection methods.
All managers are, in essence, HR managers — they must master HRM skills to build effective, motivated, and legally compliant teams.
Question 3
Explain the technological, workplace demographic, and workforce trends that have impacted HR management. Give an example of each.
Introduction
Dessler identifies major trends that continuously reshape how HR manages people, processes, and policies in modern organizations.
1 — Technological trends
  • Automation, AI, and digital tools are replacing routine jobs and changing skill requirements.
  • HR uses technology for e-recruitment, online training (e-learning), HR information systems (HRIS), and performance tracking.
  • Remote work technology (Zoom, cloud tools) has changed how HR manages productivity and collaboration.
Example
Amazon uses AI-driven recruitment software to screen thousands of applications, reducing hiring time significantly.
2 — Workplace demographic trends
  • Workforce is more diverse: multiple generations (Gen Z, Millennials, Boomers) work side by side.
  • Growing diversity in gender, ethnicity, and nationality requires HR to manage inclusion actively.
  • Aging workforce = more retirements, knowledge transfer challenges, and demand for flexible schedules.
Example
Google has diversity & inclusion programs specifically to recruit and retain women and minorities in tech roles.
3 — Workforce/economic trends
  • Rise of the gig economy: more freelancers, part-timers, and contract workers.
  • Globalization: HR must manage multinational teams across different cultures and labor laws.
  • Skills gap: rapid change means companies constantly retrain workers (upskilling/reskilling).
Example
Uber and Fiverr rely on gig workers instead of full-time employees, forcing HR to rethink benefits and engagement strategies.
These trends force HR to be more strategic, tech-savvy, inclusive, and globally aware than ever before.
Question 4
Compare the authority of line and staff managers. Give examples of each.
Definition
Authority refers to the right to make decisions, direct work, and give orders. Dessler distinguishes between two types: line authority and staff authority.
Line authority
  • Direct, chain-of-command authority — the right to direct subordinates' work.
  • Line managers make decisions and give orders that must be followed.
  • They are directly responsible for achieving the organization's core goals.
  • They have authority over employees in their own department.
Example
A production manager who directs factory workers on what to produce, when to work, and how to meet daily targets — this is line authority.
Staff authority
  • Advisory authority — staff managers advise, assist, and recommend, but cannot command.
  • Staff managers support line managers with expertise (HR, legal, accounting).
  • They have authority only within their own department's staff.
  • Line managers may accept or reject staff advice — it is not binding.
Example
An HR manager who recommends a new recruitment policy to a department head — the department head can choose whether to follow it. This is staff authority.
Key comparison
  • Line: Direct command → accountable for results → e.g., Sales Manager, Plant Manager
  • Staff: Advisory role → accountable for advice quality → e.g., HR Manager, Legal Counsel, Finance Analyst
Both types are essential: line authority drives operations, while staff authority ensures decisions are informed, legal, and strategic.
